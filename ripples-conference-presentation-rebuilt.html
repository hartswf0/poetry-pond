Coaxing the Ripples

Interactive writing becomes interesting when one action can still be felt several turns later.

Most AI systems are good at producing events. They are less good at producing a world. A character does something. The sentence disappears. The next sentence starts fresh. The language moves, but nothing has weight.

This talk is about a different ambition. Ripples asks whether a generative system can remember enough for an action to leave a mark.

Terhi Marttila, Jay David Bolter, Watson Hartsoe
01
The simplest example

The blueberry test.

A bird eats a blueberry. Two turns later, the blueberry returns: blue, round, untouched. Many AI systems do this. The prose keeps flowing. The world keeps forgetting.

Why this matters

If the system cannot keep track of one eaten berry, it cannot build a convincing ecology, mindscape, or simulation. It can only produce fresh language.

Bad version

“The bird pecks the blueberry.”

Next turn: “The blueberry glints in the grass.”

Better version

“The bird swallows the blueberry.”

Three turns later: “The earthworm moves through stained soil beneath the branch where the bird fed.”

The basic unit

Entity

Something occupies the world: a face, a stream, a root, a rumor, a memory.

The pressure

Vector

Something acts: whisper, shade, hunger, water, suspicion, decay.

The outcome

Ripple: the effect spreads beyond the first contact. That spread is what the interface has to make legible.

One root, three works

The shared prompt did not produce three copies.

It produced three branches. Each branch kept the same underlying problem—how to make an event persist—but pushed it in a different direction.

Whispers

One thought bends another. Pressure moves across a grid of minds.

Ecologies

One intervention redistributes pressure across a forest of relations.

Simulation

The system continues to update after the hand leaves the controls.

07
Branch 1

Whispers

In Bolter’s work, the unit is not an action in physical space. It is a pressure in mental space. A whisper enters one pane. The next thought in that pane changes. Adjacent panes begin to register the shift.

Concrete example

A calm line in one mind becomes guarded in the next. Nearby characters start to echo suspicion, not because anyone argued with them, but because the mood of the field changed.

The reader does not speak for the characters. The reader changes the weather around them.

09
Branch 2

Ecologies

Marttila’s work treats the world as continuous. Water, root, stone, lichen, fungus, bird. The user touches one point in that field, but the consequence travels elsewhere.

Concrete example

Add water near a pine. The soil darkens. Fungal growth improves. Shade thickens. Lichen loses ground. The later state looks quiet, but several earlier choices are folded into it.

The forest does not wait for the user. The user cuts into an ongoing system.

Branch 3

Simulation

Hartsoe’s branch asks what happens when the hand loosens and the system keeps moving. Autonomy is not enough. A clock is autonomous. The question is whether drift remains legible.

Concrete example

A moisture change triggers fungal growth. Fungal growth changes root health. Root weakness alters a neighboring plant. The user touched the first step, but the third step still has to feel earned.

The present has to look as if it grew from the past.

13
A harder lesson

Language makes matter too easily.

Mention a wolf and a wolf appears. Mention rain and every surface shines. This is one reason generated worlds can feel bloated. Nothing costs anything. Naming becomes conjuring.

Concrete example

We found that a new entity could not arrive just because its name crossed the screen. It needed a path: migration, decomposition, invitation, collision, growth.

Words could suggest matter. They could not summon it for free.

15
What changed

The sentence stopped being the unit of authorship.

In ordinary writing, the sentence is the thing you control. In Ripples, authorship moved upstream. The craft lies in deciding what may touch, what may persist, what may vanish, and how long a change remains alive.

Short version

The author no longer writes only outputs.

The author designs consequence.

16
17
Closing
A world begins when a sentence can no longer take itself back.

That is the thread connecting Whispers, Ecologies, and Simulation. Each work asks the same question in a different key: can writing become a field in which actions accumulate rather than disappear?
