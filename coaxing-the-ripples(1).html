Coaxing the Ripples

Thoughts on distant writing of interactive works

Terhi Marttila · Watson Hartsoe · Jay David Bolter
2026

RIPPLES begins with a shared worldtext system and unfolds into three distinct interactive works. This essay treats the artifacts—not the notebooks—as the primary evidence. The process record appears only where it clarifies the larger argument.

Abstract

Ripples is an experiment in what Luciano Floridi and Hannes Bajohr have described as distant writing: literary production in which the human author acts through prompts, models, procedures, and selections rather than inscribing every sentence directly. We extend this account from generated prose to interactive works. In Ripples, the author does not merely prompt a text. The author prompts an artifact that can contain language, images, sound, interface controls, executable rules, persistent state, and future opportunities for participation. A shared “worldtext” prompt produced a multi-paned interface modeled on a performance deck. Three artists then developed that common system in different aesthetic directions. The resulting works demonstrate that interactive distant writing is not best understood as remote sentence production. It is the composition of a field of possible actions and consequences.

This essay argues that the distinctive medium of interactive distant writing is conditioned emergence. Its central materials are entities, relations, constraints, delays, interfaces, memories, and transformations. Generative abundance matters, but only when the work can distinguish change from mere variation. The difficulty is therefore not simply getting a model to write beautifully. It is making a system remember that something happened, allowing that event to alter what can happen next, and shaping the interface so that a reader can perceive and perform those consequences. We call this practice coaxing: an iterative form of authorship that combines explicit structure with receptive attention, preserving human responsibility while permitting computational systems to produce transformations that were not fully specified in advance.

Keywords: distant writing; interactive digital narrative; generative AI; worldtext; operative fiction; artificial life; metaprompting; procedural authorship; human–AI co-creation

From distant writing to distant work

Distant writing begins with a displacement. The human writer no longer stands in an immediate relation to every sentence. A prompt, model, or procedure intervenes. The author writes something that writes something else. Yet this formulation becomes insufficient when the generated object is not a passage of prose but an interactive system. In that case, what emerges at a distance is not only language. It is a work that can continue to produce language, respond to intervention, reorganize its own state, and distribute attention across several media at once.

Ripples explores this enlarged condition. Its point of departure was a collective system prompt called the worldtext: a specification for a multi-paned interface on the analogy of a DJ deck. The interface gathers entities—human, nonhuman, and posthuman—into a bounded ecology. A reader-performer can select an entity, introduce a force, and observe effects as they spread through the system. The foundational image is simple: an event touches one entity, but its consequences do not stop there. They propagate. The work is therefore organized less by plot than by the transmission of difference.

Floridi’s account of distant writing emphasizes degrees of remove between authorial intention and textual output. Bajohr likewise asks us to reconsider authorship when models intervene between the writer and the written. Ripples adds another degree. The human authors prompt a system that generates an artifact; the artifact then mediates the actions of a reader; those actions update the world; the updated world conditions subsequent textual and audiovisual events. Authorship travels through a chain of operations rather than terminating in a static output.

The object of authorship is not simply a generated text. It is a situation in which texts and consequences can continue to occur.

This changes the unit of composition. A sentence remains important, but so do the conditions under which that sentence becomes possible. The interactive distant writer composes relations, thresholds, transitions, delays, interfaces, state variables, and permissions. Revision can mean rewriting a description, but it can also mean changing who is allowed to act, how long an effect persists, whether an entity can disappear, what counts as adjacency, or what the system must remember before it speaks again. The work is authored not only at the level of expression but at the level of possibility.

Worldtext: writing the state of a world

The term worldtext names both a conceptual object and a practical design strategy. It is conceptual because it treats language as a way of making a world’s relations legible. It is practical because the text displayed by the interface functions as a state-sensitive readout. A worldtext sentence should not be detachable decoration. It should arise from what an entity is, what has happened to it, what surrounds it, and what pressures are currently moving through the system.

This distinguishes worldtext from conventional narrative generation. A conventional story system often privileges plot progression: one event follows another, and the value of each event lies in how it advances an arc. Ripples instead begins from relational presence. A blackbird, stream, lichen, pine, boulder, earthworm, owl, and sun may occupy the same ecology without becoming characters in a single human-centered drama. Their significance lies in their partial perspectives, capacities, dependencies, and vulnerabilities. The system asks not only “what happens next?” but “what does this event become from here?”

The worldtext is therefore neither a transparent map of an objective world nor a claim to reproduce a nonhuman interior. It is an operative speculation. The project draws on Jakob von Uexküll’s concept of the Umwelt, the perceptual world available to a particular organism, while retaining an explicit epistemic humility. We cannot know what a stream, lichen, or blackbird experiences in itself. We can, however, construct disciplined fictions that make our assumptions about their relations available for examination.

Worldtext is most successful when it holds two commitments together. First, the writing should be sensuous enough to make a relation felt. Second, the relation should remain accountable to the system’s state. Without the first, the interface collapses into a diagnostic dashboard. Without the second, it becomes a poetry machine whose descriptions float free of consequence. The challenge of the project has been to keep language and world mutually answerable.

The distinction is crucial: a state readout need not sound technical. It can be lyrical, compressed, fragmented, or sonorous. What makes it a readout is not its style but its dependency on state.

In practical terms, this means separating the event model from its verbal realization. “The deer eats the blueberry” is first a transformation: the blueberry changes status, the deer changes status, and other entities may inherit consequences. Only then should the system decide how that transformation becomes language. The prose may vary; the event cannot remain optional. If the text says that the blueberry was eaten but the blueberry remains fully available in the next turn, language has ceased to describe a world. It has become an improvisational overlay.

Operative ecology: entity, vector, ripple

Ripples can be reduced to an operative triad: entity, vector, ripple. An entity is a situated participant in the system. A vector is a directed pressure or intervention. A ripple is the redistribution of effects through the ecology. This triad is more fundamental than any particular interface skin, genre, or prose style. It provides the work with a grammar of change.

The entities are deliberately heterogeneous. They can be animate, inanimate, environmental, technological, or abstract. This heterogeneity prevents the system from assuming that every participant should behave like a human character with a desire, obstacle, and voice. A pine tree and a water drop can both matter without sharing an identical psychology. Their differences should be expressed through distinct capacities: absorption, obstruction, erosion, shelter, predation, illumination, decay, transport, or transformation.

Vectors provide a compact way to steer those capacities. The early shared system organized interventions into three canonical forms:

Goal

A directional tendency: growth, approach, nourishment, shelter, continuation, or attraction.

Obstacle

A resistance or constraint: blockage, scarcity, predation, incompatibility, damage, or delay.

Shift

A change in relation or condition: mutation, migration, dissolution, alliance, emergence, or reorientation.

These categories are not plot buttons. They are pressures applied to a local configuration. A goal can become an obstacle for a neighboring entity; an obstacle can redirect a stream and create a habitat; a shift can dissolve one entity into the conditions of another. The important question is not which label was selected but how the system translates that intervention into redistributed possibilities.

Here the project approaches Harun Farocki’s idea of the operative image: an image that participates in an operation rather than merely depicting one. The worldtext interface is operative in a comparable sense. Its panes, labels, logs, and animations are not illustrations placed around a hidden simulation. They are the means by which a world is selected, acted upon, and made available for further action. The text performs work inside the system: it records, cues, constrains, and redirects.

This is why “ripple” cannot remain a decorative concentric animation. Visual rings may help a reader notice that an event is spreading, but the deeper ripple is a state transition that survives the animation. A stream diverted by a boulder should later carry different matter. A berry eaten by a deer should no longer wait untouched for another eater. A shadow displaced by an artificial light should alter the perceptual situation of neighboring entities. A ripple is not the appearance of consequence. It is consequence with duration.

The world jockey and the interface as score

The shared interface was modeled on a DJ deck because the metaphor relocates authorship from inscription to performance. A DJ does not compose every sound in the moment, yet neither do they merely press play. They prepare materials, read a changing situation, choose transitions, control intensities, and make timing consequential. Ripples gives the reader a related role: the world jockey.

The world jockey does not enter the ecology as its heroic protagonist. Instead, they manipulate relationships among entities whose lives do not exist solely for the user’s benefit. They may focus attention on one perspective, inject a vector, allow the system to run automatically, or intervene again before a previous effect has settled. The interface becomes a score for action: not a sequence that must be followed, but a structured field of possible performances.

This performance model clarifies why interface design is inseparable from writing. Labels such as “do,” “ripple,” “goal,” or “obstacle” frame what the user believes they are authoring. A button that says “generate” suggests production of new content; a button that says “intervene” suggests transformation of an existing world. A grid can imply equivalence among entities, while a central portrait can quietly restore the hierarchy the project hoped to escape. The spatial arrangement of panes, the visibility of history, and the pace of animation all participate in the work’s rhetoric.

Three operating modes—manual, semi-manual, and automatic—further distribute agency. In manual mode, the user becomes an explicit mediator of every event. In semi-manual mode, the system elaborates after a human intervention. In automatic mode, the ecology continues with less direct guidance. These are not simply convenience settings. They stage three different relationships between author, performer, and generative process.

Manual

The performer bears visible responsibility for each intervention. Causality is legible, but emergence may feel constrained.

Semi-manual

The performer supplies a pressure and the system distributes it. This mode most clearly dramatizes coaxing.

Automatic

The ecology continues beyond immediate command. Surprise increases, but accountability and readability can weaken.

Mixed performance

The strongest form may move among all three, using automation as duration rather than as abdication.

Under this model, the interface is not a neutral wrapper for a language model. It is an argument about participation. It says that reading can be a form of intervention, that authorship can be distributed without becoming ownerless, and that a generative system should expose enough of its operation for a performer to develop judgment rather than merely request novelty.

When a ripple becomes real

The most important lesson of the project is that emergence depends less on verbal surprise than on temporal structure. A model can generate an unexpected sentence at every turn while the world itself remains unchanged. Such a system looks abundant but feels shallow. It offers perpetual novelty without history.

“A lot of the magic you want comes from time-lagged consequences. That is where the ripples become real. Without delayed state, the system will keep preferring immediate drama.”

Model feedback recorded during development, April 2026

The observation names a central distinction. Immediate drama is easy for a language model. It can turn almost any prompt into collision, revelation, danger, or emotional intensity. Delayed consequence is harder because it requires a persistent representation of what has changed and a rule for when that change becomes perceptible. The system must carry forward latent effects rather than regenerate the present from a fresh paragraph.

A credible ripple therefore needs at least four components: an event, an affected state, a propagation rule, and a later opportunity for that changed state to matter. Consider a boulder blocking the roots of a pine. The immediate event is obstruction. The pine’s state may change toward stress or lateral growth. The propagation rule may alter soil moisture, fungal relations, or the path of a water drop. The later opportunity arrives when another event encounters those altered conditions. Only then does the earlier obstruction become more than a descriptive flourish.

Persistence also disciplines entity creation. During development, a recurring failure appeared when incidental words could become new entities. A description might mention a lantern, and suddenly the lantern entered the world as though language had summoned matter without cost or pathway. This made the system lively in the short term but weakened its ecology.

“Right now mention can become matter, which weakens the world. New entities should arrive through ecological pathways, not incidental wording.”

Model feedback recorded during development, April 2026

That principle applies beyond entity spawning. Every transformation should have a pathway. Eating, absorption, birth, erosion, decomposition, illumination, and migration are not synonyms for generic change. They carry different conservation rules and different implications for what remains. If a water drop is absorbed by a pine, it may cease to exist as an independent entity while continuing as moisture within another state. If a bird eats an ant, the disappearance of the ant is not a deletion from the story but a transfer within the ecology.

Interactive distant writing becomes distinctive at precisely this level. The prompt does not only ask a model to describe an event. It defines the operational grammar by which events acquire duration. In a literary text, an author may imply continuity through narration. In an interactive system, continuity must also be implemented. The gap between those two forms of continuity is where much of the real authorial labor resides.

Getting closer to aesthetic intent

Distant writing promises abundance, but abundance is not the same as aesthetic range. Generative models repeatedly fall into a recognizable house style: fluent, atmospheric, emotionally legible, and vaguely elevated. In Ripples, requests for sensual, ecological, or nonhuman writing often produced soft abstraction, personification, melodramatic verbs, and metaphors that converted physical relation into human feeling. The language seemed “poetic” because it displayed the symptoms of poetry, not because it had discovered a precise form for the event.

The problem cannot be solved by adding adjectives to the prompt. “More strange,” “more lyrical,” “less human,” or “more embodied” remain invitations for the model to intensify familiar patterns. Aesthetic direction becomes effective only when it is translated into observable constraints. Should entities speak in first person or third? Can they ask questions? How many sensory channels may a description invoke? Must every sentence refer to a state variable? How often may the system use analogy? Which verbs are forbidden because they falsely psychologize an inanimate process?

This translation from taste into constraint is one of the central skills of distant authorship. Taste is often treated as an ineffable faculty: the artist recognizes what feels right. In generative practice, recognition remains necessary, but it is not enough. The author must also identify why an output fails and convert that judgment into a new condition for production. The farther the writing moves from direct inscription, the more explicit the author must become about the operations hidden inside preference.

A practical distinction

Stylistic prompting asks for an effect: “make it uncanny, sensual, and ecological.” Operative prompting specifies behaviors that can produce or prevent that effect: restrict anthropomorphic interiority; bind every description to a sensed gradient; allow one concrete transformation per passage; prohibit rhetorical questions; preserve the entity’s scale and available senses.

Yet constraint alone is not the answer. Too many rules produce obedient lifelessness. Too few rules return the model to generic fluency. The productive pattern in Ripples was scaffolding followed by release: establish the ontology, event rules, perspective limits, and memory structure firmly, then permit the model greater freedom at the level of expression. The system should be strict about what happened and exploratory about how that happening is rendered.

This division also allows local models, remote models, templates, and hand-written fragments to coexist. During development, a local Llama model produced weaker “rippling” than larger remote systems, but the experiment clarified that generative intelligence should not be treated as a single interchangeable substance. Different models bring different rhythms, costs, context limits, and stylistic defaults. The work’s design must account for these material differences rather than hiding them beneath a universal “AI” label.

The evocative moments remain important. A phrase sometimes appears that gives a relation exactly the pressure or strangeness it needed. A model may also identify a structural flaw that the artist had understood only as dissatisfaction. Such moments explain the attraction of the process. They are not proof that the model possesses aesthetic intention. They are occasions on which a computational transformation becomes useful to human judgment.

Three works from one worldtext

The shared prompt did not function as a blueprint for a single ideal artifact. It established a possibility space. Each artist then pushed the worldtext toward a different aesthetic and operative emphasis, producing three works that remain recognizably related while refusing visual or experiential uniformity.

This divergence is the strongest evidence for distant writing’s creative abundance. The abundance does not lie in generating hundreds of interchangeable outputs. It lies in the capacity of one conceptual system to support distinct interpretations. One branch may foreground the interface as a performance instrument; another may intensify nonhuman perspective and speculative ecology; another may treat text as terrain, state display, or material field. Each work reveals a different implication already latent in the common structure.

The three works therefore should not be judged by fidelity to a master prompt. A generative prompt is not a sacred original whose descendants become weaker through deviation. Its value is demonstrated by the transformations it can sustain. A strong shared system gives collaborators enough common structure to remain in conversation and enough openness to produce genuinely different answers.

This also refines the role of metaprompting. The master prompt produced further prompts, and those prompts helped produce interfaces and code. But the process was not a clean hierarchy in which an initial instruction controlled everything below it. Later discoveries altered earlier assumptions. A useful mechanism found in one artifact clarified a missing concept in the shared system. A failure in another exposed an ambiguity that had been concealed by the master prompt’s apparent coherence. The relation between levels was recursive: specification generated artifacts, and artifacts revised specification.

What unites the final works is not a common appearance but a common question: how can an interface make relation felt as an event rather than merely describe relation as a theme? Their differences are not noise around that question. They are the method by which the question becomes visible from several positions.

Reflections from practice

The process notebooks matter, but they matter as evidence for the preceding claims rather than as the paper’s organizing center. Read in retrospect, their scattered questions cluster around four persistent problems: how to make the text interesting, how to make consequences evident, how to keep entities coherent, and how to prevent prompting from dissolving the project’s original purpose.

The notes repeatedly return to reduction: what is the smallest system in which a ripple can be perceived? Two entities may be enough when their relation persists. Nine entities may be too many when each is only a rotating paragraph. A rich interface may be causally empty; a sparse one may feel alive if an event is remembered and reappears indirectly several turns later. The search for a “bare minimum” was therefore not merely a production strategy. It became a way of testing the concept itself.

The notebooks also record the peculiar temporality of agentic creation. Each prompt initiates a period of waiting, followed by the need to inspect a large change whose internal decisions may be difficult to reconstruct. Long model runs can exceed usage limits or stop midway through a code edit. A small request can unexpectedly reorganize several files. The author alternates between command, suspension, surprise, diagnosis, and repair.

This rhythm has an affective and economic dimension. In one development conversation, the process was described as “very addictive,” “like a drug,” and potentially “worse than social media.” A weekly limit or a twenty-dollar expenditure becomes part of the aesthetic situation because it shapes which branches are pursued, abandoned, or compressed. Distant writing is not immaterial creativity. It is conditioned by subscriptions, context windows, local hardware, network permissions, model availability, and the stamina required to read what the system has produced.

These conditions make discernment increasingly important. The central creative skill is not the ability to issue the largest number of prompts. It is knowing what to pursue, what to refuse, what deserves another iteration, and when generated complexity has begun to obscure the work rather than deepen it. The model can produce options faster than the artist can form a reason to choose among them. Taste becomes valuable not as a finishing polish but as a scarce capacity for direction.

Coaxing as a method

We use the word coaxing because neither “automation” nor “collaboration” adequately describes the practice. Automation suggests a transfer of labor toward efficiency. Collaboration suggests partners with relatively stable identities and shared understanding. Coaxing names a more asymmetric relation: repeated invitation, resistance, redirection, receptivity, and judgment across a system whose capacities are powerful but opaque.

Coaxing is not a euphemism for vague prompting. It has a disciplined structure. The author first makes the world explicit: what exists, what can change, what must persist, and what the system may never infer merely from a phrase. The author then permits variation within those conditions, reads the results at several scales, identifies where behavior and intention diverge, and revises the conditions rather than only correcting the surface.

Write the ontology

Define entities by capacities, relations, and state—not only by descriptive names.

Write the transition

Specify what an action changes and how that change persists.

Write the delay

Give consequences time to travel before demanding another spectacle.

Write the interface

Treat controls, panes, logs, and pacing as part of the work’s language.

Write the limits

Prevent mention from becoming matter and fluency from replacing coherence.

Read the system

Evaluate patterns across many outputs, not only the most attractive sentence.

At its best, coaxing preserves two forms of agency at once. It preserves human responsibility because the artists define the stakes, recognize failure, select what becomes public, and remain answerable for the system’s implications. It preserves computational alterity because the model is not reduced to a deterministic template. The system can still produce a relation, phrase, or technical proposal that the authors did not anticipate.

The balance is delicate. If structure becomes total, the model contributes only ornament. If openness becomes total, the work collapses into uncurated variation. Coaxing occupies the interval between those failures. It builds enough structure that divergence remains meaningful and enough openness that the work can still exceed its specification.

Coaxing the ripples

Ripples began with a formal image: an impact spreading from one entity to others in a closed system. The experiment ultimately revealed that this image describes its authorship as well as its fictional worlds. A concept enters a shared prompt. The prompt becomes a generative interface. The interface affords performances. Performances alter state. State conditions later language. Later language changes how the artists understand the concept they began with. The method repeats the metaphor.

Interactive distant writing does not simply move the writer farther away from a finished sentence. It relocates writing across several layers of a work. The author writes the world’s entities, the pressures that can act on them, the rules by which effects propagate, the interfaces through which a reader intervenes, and the expressive systems that render change perceptible. The distance is real, but it does not remove authorship. It makes authorship architectural.

The difficulty of the practice lies in the fact that generative systems prefer the immediate. They readily offer a new phrase, image, event, or dramatic turn. A convincing interactive work must insist on duration. It must remember what has happened, preserve what has been transformed, and allow prior events to return as altered conditions rather than repeated exposition. A ripple becomes real when the system carries an event beyond the moment in which it was generated.

The promise of the practice lies in its multiplicity. From one worldtext, three different works could emerge—not because the prompt contained an ideal form, but because it established a relational field capable of sustaining interpretation. Distant authorship becomes artistically meaningful when it produces such consequential divergence: not an endless supply of outputs, but several distinct works that make the shared problem newly visible.

To coax is to work inside that tension. It is to state enough, observe closely, refuse generic fluency, and remain open to transformation. It is to give the system a world rather than merely a topic, a memory rather than merely a context window, and consequences rather than merely continuation. The most important question is no longer whether the machine can write. It is whether authors and machines can construct an interactive world in which writing has somewhere to go—and something to change when it arrives.

Terhi Marttila

Terhi Marttila is an artist, researcher, and lecturer. She holds a PhD in Digital Media from the University of Porto and was a postdoctoral fellow at the Interactive Technologies Institute and eGames Lab from 2023 to 2025. Her digital artworks have appeared on platforms and at venues including raum.pt, The New River, Nokturno, Taper, ISEA, ELO, ICIDS, DIS, and Videojogos. Her work is included in the Electronic Literature Collection 4.

Watson Hartsoe

Watson Hartsoe is a researcher and computational arts practitioner at the Georgia Institute of Technology. His work explores generative AI, interactive media, and cultural systems theory, focusing on how computational systems simulate complex human and ecological expressions. He develops generative pipelines, interactive simulations, and toolchains that bridge technical AI development with experimental digital storytelling and artificial life.

Jay David Bolter

Jay David Bolter is emeritus professor and former co-director of the Augmented Environments Lab at the Georgia Institute of Technology. His books include Remediation, with Richard Grusin, and The Digital Plenitude. His current research and creative focus is generative AI as a medium of expression.
